# Rift Rules

This page is the designated deep treatment of the central phenomenon. Rifts are magic,
not speculative physics; depth here means consistent behaviour and gameplay consequences,
not a scientific explanation.

## Basis — temporary magic

- Rifts are temporary openings between worlds. They can form naturally or be called by a
  trained caster. [current call]
- Weather is a [tentative] working metaphor for unpredictable conditions that can be
  read and influenced. It is not a settled cause, origin, or complete rules model.
- There is no hidden technological explanation and no later science-fiction reveal.
  [current call]

## Anchors

- Anchors are unexplained ancient magical stones or ruins that attract and stabilize
  Rifts. [current call]
- A world may contain several Anchors.
- Separate guilds may claim different Anchors on one world as capped home sites.
- Claiming an Anchor changes local ownership, building, and return rights, not its
  magical behaviour.
- Guilds already sharing a world need no Rift to approach or attack each other's homes.
- An Anchor is an arrival focus, not necessarily an exact spawn point; arrival control is
  [open].

## Casting

- Trained people can cast a spell that calls or opens a Rift. [current call]
- The spell may bias a Rift using knowledge or evidence of another world, but the exact
  targeting rule is [open].
- Calling and sustaining a Rift has a cost; its resource and scaling are [open].
- The casting sequence is long enough to telegraph the act and provision the destination
  world.

## Duration and commitment

- Rifts close. Their lifetime bounds travel, expeditions, and raids.
- A committed hostile Rift cannot be cancelled by defender logout or immediate closure.
- Collected loot remains carried and loseable until its carrier returns through a valid
  Rift; deeper expedition progress offers better rewards.
- Exact duration, interruption, and return travel are [open].

## Arrival and anti-bunkering

- Cross-world attackers may approach through different Anchors; same-world neighbours
  approach through ordinary geography.
- Fortifying one Anchor cannot guarantee safety from every hostile Rift.
- Defenses protect the settlement and surrounding terrain, not a single permanent
  doorway.
- Exact Anchor selection, arrival warning, protection, no-build clearance, and concurrent
  hostile Rifts are [open].

## Exposure

- Calling, crossing, fighting around, and repeatedly using Rifts leaves evidence for
  cross-world targeting.
- More Anchors and more activity create more reach and more possible exposure.
- Old evidence loses targeting value; geography may remain known without granting
  permanent hostile access from another world. Same-world neighbours need no such access.
- Exact player-facing signs and the spell's use of them are [open].

## World and server boundary

- Each destination is a bounded world, not a seamless universe.
- Separate simulations resolve against one shared durable history.
- A world may place concurrent visitors in the same live geography. Every live
  simulation has a player budget; what happens when that budget is full is [open].
- A body disconnected during danger remains authoritative indefinitely. If the world
  sleeps, the body is stored with its durable state and restored when the world next
  becomes active.
- A Rift transfers players, creatures, and permitted cargo between world authorities.
- Same-world travel and attacks remain under that world's authority and require no Rift
  transfer.
- Casting covers destination startup; closure bounds contested lifetime.
- Terrain generation does not require Anchor-centered topology or a permanent
  Anchor-to-base corridor.

## Open

- Blind casting and whether it can reach claimed worlds.
- Target-world versus target-Anchor control.
- Arrival selection and simultaneous connections.
- Full-world concurrency behaviour.
- Casting cost, mass/cargo limits, duration, return route, and failure.
- Why Rifts exist and whether anyone made the Anchors.
