# World

The Gaters world bible in one file. Mechanics live in [[systems]]; the central
phenomenon's constraints live in [[rift-rules|Rift Rules]]; unresolved questions live
in [[questions]].

## Premise

- **Rifts** are temporary magical openings between fantasy worlds. Some form naturally,
  and trained people can call them with a spell. [current call]
- Treating their natural formation as storms or magical weather is a [tentative]
  metaphor, not settled lore.
- Ancient **Anchors** attract and stabilize Rifts. They are unexplained magical stones or
  ruins; a world may hold several. [current call]
- Players explore, gather, tame, build, and raid across bounded procedural worlds. The
  target experience is **Valheim + Solo Leveling + ARK**: survival-craft exploration,
  escalating Rift threats, creature mastery, and vulnerable player settlements.
- Claiming an Anchor establishes a guild's ownership, building rights, and return point
  for its local home territory. It does **not** privatize the world, claim other Anchors,
  or change how Rifts behave. [current call]
- Reach and wealth require opening Rifts and venturing out. That activity leaves evidence
  outsiders can use to hunt toward the actor's world: **cross-world reach equals
  exposure**. [current call]

## Design thesis — reach equals exposure

- A quiet home is hard to reach from another world, not invulnerable. Neighbours on the
  same world can travel to and attack it directly, including while its owners are
  offline. [current call]
- Acting outward creates routes, witnesses, traces, and enemies that can lead back.
- More Anchors create more reach and more possible ways into a world; one fortified
  doorway cannot solve defense.
- PvE, PvP, trade, scouting, and conquest share one universe. Rift use controls
  cross-world exposure; claiming a home on a shared world accepts persistent neighbour
  risk rather than selecting a separate PvP server.
- Homes do not decay or demand upkeep merely because owners are absent. This does not
  prevent same-world neighbours from attacking. [current call]

## What can be lost

- **Never lost:** the player's continuing identity and permanent knowledge.
- **Mortal:** the current body, carried gear, creatures, structures, holdings, and a
  claimed home once attackers reach and breach it.
- The fiction behind death and return is [[questions|open]]; the gameplay floor is that
  bodies and civilizations can be lost without deleting the player.

## Worlds

- Every world is a bounded, persistent place with ordinary geography. There is no
  player-facing server browser.
- The universe has one shared history: durable ownership and world changes do not split
  into competing server copies. A live world may be shared geography for its concurrent
  visitors, within a bounded player budget. [current call]
- **Unclaimed worlds** are the exploration frontier: gather, hunt, tame, discover
  Anchors, find settlements, and claim a home.
- A shared world may contain several guild homes, each centered on a separately claimed
  Anchor. Home count, residents, and active visitors are capped by world budgets.
- Worlds with claimed Anchors use the same Rift rules as unclaimed worlds. Ownership
  changes local building and return rights, not who nature permits to approach.
- A world may hold several Anchors far apart. Players travel between them by ordinary
  movement; land, sea, and flying creatures can make that geography meaningful.
- Worlds exist cheaply as persistent records and are provisioned for play when a Rift
  opens or players are present.

## Rifts and Anchors

- Rifts are **magic**, not disguised technology. There is no later science-fiction
  explanation. [current call]
- Natural Rifts create accidental discovery. A trained caster can call a Rift, but exact
  targeting, cost, and arrival control remain [[questions|open]].
- Anchors catch and stabilize Rifts. A caster may influence the destination, but the
  current model does not yet decide whether they choose a world, an Anchor, or only a
  direction.
- Multiple Anchors are intentional attack surfaces. Defenders protect their settlement
  and territory, not one permanent portal room.
- Rifts deliver cross-world attackers; neighbouring guilds already on the world approach
  by ordinary travel.
- Rifts close. Their temporary lifetime bounds cross-world expeditions and raids;
  same-world neighbour conflict is not bound by a Rift.

## Ownership and groups

- A **guild** owns each home Anchor and local territory it claims; a solo player is a
  guild of one. [current call]
- Several guilds may own separate home Anchors on the same world. Claiming one does not
  claim the world or its remaining Anchors. [current call]
- Guilds sharing a world may attack each other's homes directly; ownership grants build
  and return rights, not immunity. [current call]
- Claiming does not change Rift or Anchor behaviour or grant magical transit immunity.
- The institution, ritual, or shared record that recognizes ownership is [open]. Do not
  invent a replacement government until the mechanic needs one.

## Themes and tone

- Mythic frontier fantasy: beautiful wilderness, dangerous creatures, ruined Anchors,
  temporary Rifts, ambitious guilds, and escalating unknown worlds.
- Start grounded in survival tools, handmade settlements, and taming; climb toward
  stronger bodies, creatures, equipment, and Rift magic.
- Magic remains magic. Ancient Anchors may be mysterious without becoming machines.
- Accidental discovery is core. Worlds are places to enter and explore, not activities
  selected from a menu.

## Not yet established

- Named peoples, factions, religions, myths, languages, characters, and gods.
- Why Rifts exist, who made the Anchors, or whether anyone made them.
- The player's origin, the metaphysics of returning after death, and the ownership
  authority.
